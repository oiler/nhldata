First, familiarize yourself with the resources we began this project with - specifically the NHL API Documentation doc in the resources/ folder.

The public NHL data landscape has spent a lot of time on creating metrics around expected goals. I believe that hockeyviz.com is a leader in this space.
His latest work is published here:
- https://hockeyviz.com/txt/xg8
- https://hockeyviz.com/txt/sG
- https://hockeyviz.com/txt/magnus9EV

I want to build on this research, and see if adding my own analysis and research can help move this work forward.

Specifically, i believe in these concepts
- shooter handedness plays a massive role in shot quality. for example, a left shot who takes a shot on the right side of the ice has a better angle to pick their spot. a left shooter on the right side is also more likely to be able to get the shot off more quickly, eg as a one-timer. One timer shots are not recorded in the nhl play by play data. but shooting from your offwing could be a stronger indication of this and overall shot quality.
- shot type matters a lot. I'm looking to measure pure shooting quality, and that mostly happens when taking a wrist shot or a snap shot have more of a shooter's intent
- shot location can be inconsistently logged, but for the most part, high quality scoring chances come from the middle of the ice and well below the blue line
- who the shooter is matters a lot. is the shooter a forward or defenseman? does the shooter have a history of scoring goals? does the shooter play a lot of minutes? 

So what happens when we isolate shot data to these kinds of scenarios. Can we isolate a certain kind of shot quality that brings in all of these factors?

## Research summary (Claude, 2026-06-10)

Findings from a review of the three hockeyviz articles above, an audit of our local data, and a prior-art sweep of public hockey analytics.

### Headline finding

hockeyviz's xG8 is structurally blind to the handedness hypothesis: McCurdy symmetrizes shot locations left-right across the center line and states that "if there are non-trivial left-right disparities at the league level I will not discern them." Neither sG nor Magnus 9 touches handedness. The canonical public xG models (MoneyPuck, Evolving-Hockey, TopDownHockey) also exclude it — MoneyPuck ships an `offWing` column in its public shot data but does not list it as a model feature. The only model-level implementation found is a 2025 hobbyist blog (hockey-statistics.com "BoxID"), which reported handedness-relative location as its most important feature.

### Hypothesis-by-hypothesis novelty

| Hypothesis | Prior art | Novelty |
|---|---|---|
| Handedness / off-wing | Effect established directionally (Parnass 2016, PP only: 7.1% vs 6.1% Corsi sh%; Matt Cane: 9.9% vs 8.6%; Bonnet et al. 2024). Absent from all canonical xG models. | High — known effect, unbuilt feature |
| One-timer proxy | Every direct measurement (Stimson 2017, Valiquette's royal road, Novet 2019) required manual video tracking. No published one-timer proxy from public pbp. | Highest — and hardest |
| Shot type | In every model since Ryder 2004; central to xG8 (one location surface per shot type). Shot-type mix as a *talent signature* is barely touched (descriptive clustering only). | Low as a feature; mix-as-signal open |
| Location | Backbone of every model for 20 years. | None alone |
| Shooter identity | Heavily worked: Sprigings/Toumi 2015, MoneyPuck Bayesian multipliers, xG8 first-class shooter terms, Donnelly et al. 2025. | Low as a feature; mechanism decomposition open |

Two xG8 details support the doc's instincts: McCurdy excludes shooter identity for tips/deflections because it improves prediction (consistent with wrist/snap being where shooter skill expresses), and Parnass's argued mechanism for the off-wing edge is exactly the one stated here — one-timer availability and angle to pick a spot.

### Methodological caution

Filtering to wrist/snap shots from the slot by proven scorers will mechanically produce high shooting percentage — selection, not discovery. The defensible design controls for location, shot type, and context, then tests whether off-wing adds predictive power on top. Shooter-history inputs (past goals, minutes) make a shot-quality model circular for evaluating shooters — the standard critique of Sprigings 2015, and why Evolving-Hockey abandoned their shooter term. Decide early whether the question is predicting goals or isolating skill.

### Data readiness

- 1,312 pbp games in `data/2025/plays/`; shot events carry `shotType`, `xCoord`/`yCoord`, `zoneCode`, `situationCode`, `homeTeamDefendingSide`.
- Handedness is not in pbp, but `data/2025/players/` holds 1,024 player-landing files with `shootsCatches` (zero missing shooters in a 131-game sample). Off-wing is computable now: normalize coordinates via `homeTeamDefendingSide` + `eventOwnerTeamId`, join `shootsCatches`.
- Gaps: blocked shots never carry `shotType`; tip/deflection locations are mis-recorded by the league; recorded locations carry arena scorer bias (relevant for cross-team comparisons); no pass events exist in pbp, so one-timers must be proxied.

### Suggested first step

Replicate Parnass at even strength with public data: 5v5 off-wing vs strong-side conversion splits from local pbp, controlled by shot type and coarse location bin. His study was power-play-only and a decade old; the even-strength public-data version is unpublished. Cheap to run, validates the core hypothesis before modeling, and is a contribution on its own. The one-timer proxy (time-since-last-event × off-wing × cross-ice prior event) is the bigger prize but should follow the descriptive splits.

## Results: 5v5 off-wing vs strong-side splits (Claude, 2026-06-10)

The first experiment suggested above — the even-strength Parnass replication — has been run and adversarially verified (clean-room recomputation matched all numbers exactly; sign conventions confirmed empirically; results stress-tested against distance, angle, rebound, and shot-definition confounds).

**Sample**: 71,611 unblocked 5v5 (`situationCode 1551`) offensive-zone wrist/snap/slap/backhand shots across 1,312 games of 2025-26 play-by-play. Off-wing = shot taken from the side of the ice opposite the shooter's handedness, after rotating coordinates so every shooter attacks +x. Handedness joined from player-landing files (zero missing shooters). Convention sanity check: defensemen shoot 77.3% strong-side, consistent across both rink ends.

### Headline replicates, but it's mostly geography

Raw split: **off-wing 7.38% vs strong-side 5.26% Fenwick sh%** (+2.1pp, z = 11.5) — Parnass's power-play finding replicates at even strength. But off-wing shots are taken from much better spots (mean 30.9 ft vs 39.0 ft, and more central even within distance bands). Controlling for geometry progressively removes the edge:

| Control | Mantel-Haenszel OR (off vs strong) | p |
|---|---|---|
| Distance bands × shot type × position | 1.154 | <0.0001 |
| + 15° angle bands | 1.094 | 0.007 |
| + fine geometry (5ft × 5°) | 1.062 | 0.074 |
| All controls stacked (SOG-only, rebounds excluded, 2.5ft × 2.5°) | 1.032 | 0.42 |

The decline is monotonic and the CMH numerator falls from ~132 excess off-wing goals to ~50 — disappearing signal, not just lost power. **Conclusion: given identical location and shot type, off-wing conversion is statistically indistinguishable from strong-side** (a small true edge of OR ≤ ~1.05 cannot be ruled out; the point estimate never reverses). Rebound contamination (off-wing shots are more rebound-heavy, 13.0% vs 10.1%, purely via distance composition) and the Fenwick-vs-SOG choice do not affect the conclusions.

### The robust finding: a close-range reversal

Inside 15 feet, off-wing converts **worse**: −1.7pp (p ≈ 0.045–0.049 under both Fenwick and SOG-only definitions), negative in every angle bin, strengthening under every control. The positive edge concentrates at mid-range (30–45 ft), strongest for snap shots (+3.1pp raw). The effect has structure — a sign flip by distance — not a flat off-wing bonus.

### Interpretation

Two honest readings. Per-shot: off-wing players do not convert better from the same spot. Total: off-wing players systematically get to better spots (closer, more central) — and if blade-toward-center geometry is *why* they reach those spots, controlling for exact location absorbs part of the real mechanism. For the xG-feature question this doc poses: a flat `offWing` term adds little at 5v5, but an **off-wing × distance interaction** (negative in tight, positive mid-range) reflects real structure. This analysis is blind to one-timers (no pass events in pbp), so Parnass's PP-only result is not contradicted — the power play is likely where the one-timer mechanism pays most.

Biggest remaining confound: shooter-quality selection — elite finishers are deployed off-wing disproportionately, which inflates even the raw gap. **Natural next step: a within-shooter design** (do the same players convert better on their off-wing shots?), which removes shooter-quality selection entirely.

## Results: plotting-bias robustness check (Claude, 2026-06-10)

Question raised: is the recorded shot location displaced toward the shooter's blade side (e.g., ±2 ft by handedness × side), which would contaminate the fine-geometry control above? Three findings (`tools/plotting_bias_probe.py`, math independently verified):

- **Identifiability**: under mirrored deployment, a uniform blade-side displacement leaves the recorded L and R distributions exact mirror images for any δ — it is provably invisible to any test on pooled marginals. Detection requires within-shooter designs, rink anchors, or tracking data.
- **Within-shooter mirror gaps** (same player, strong-side minus off-wing mean |y|): −0.08 ft at 0–15 ft, +0.73 at 15–30, +2.14 at 30–45, +6.93 at 45+. A fixed ±2 ft displacement predicts a positive gap at every distance and is rejected at ≥8 SE (robust to distributional shape/folding effects).
- **Release-point story refuted**: scorers plotting the puck (offset scaling with windup) predicts gap(slap) ≫ gap(snap) > gap(wrist) at fixed distance; observed ordering is wrist ≥ snap > slap — the opposite — and D gaps ≫ F gaps at the same distance, which shot mechanics cannot produce.

Conclusion: the handedness-linked lateral structure is real positioning, not recording artifact; any plotting displacement is bounded well under 1 ft. The geometry-control verdict and the close-range reversal stand. EDGE goal sprites (tracked puck vs recorded location on goals) remain the definitive but now low-priority check.

## Results: forwards only — spatial map and one-timer proxies (Claude, 2026-06-11)

Follow-up question: isolating forwards (C/W) in natural forward territory (54 ≤ x ≤ 89, below the tops of the circles; 37,148 shots), is off-wing shooting more accurate, and where — looking for one-timer indicators or an angle sweet spot. Built on a handedness-mirrored frame (u > 0 = off-wing side, so each cell compares against its exact geometric mirror) with new prior-event fields in the per-shot table (time since last event, prior event type/team/location — spot-checked 10/10 against raw pbp).

### Headline result: the conversion edge is location, all the way down

The raw forwards edge looks strong — off-wing 9.62% vs strong-side 8.20% Fenwick sh% (+1.42pp, z = 4.8), and the *same 395 players* convert +1.39pp better on their off-wing (≥15 shots per wing). But the decisive check — same player, same geometry — collapses it: each forward's off-wing shots are taken **~3.0 ft closer and ~2.8 ft more central** than his own strong-side shots; a location baseline predicts 87% of the within-shooter gap, leaving **+0.18pp (t = 0.54, CI [−0.49, +0.84])**. Goal-given-on-net collapses identically (+1.79pp → +0.26pp). Four control variants agree.

**Verified reframe: forwards do not convert better from their off-wing — they reach better shooting locations when on it.** The ~3 ft of positional access (within-player, robust) is the off-wing advantage. Handedness belongs in the shot-generation/location column of an xG framework (Magnus-style rate/location feature), not as a finishing term.

### The spatial map: suggestive pattern, nothing survives multiplicity

12 of 16 mirrored cells positive (p = 0.077); zero of 32 cell tests survive Benjamini-Hochberg FDR at q = 0.10. The directional pattern — deficit in the tight slot lane, mild positive at mid-range and the boards — is consistent with all earlier findings but unconfirmable on one season of forward shots. Powering the map needs additional seasons.

### One-timer hypothesis: untested, not refuted — public pbp cannot see it

- The "off-wing shots follow quick plays 30% more often" signal (13.7% vs 10.6%) is 80% location composition (quick shots happen close to the net; off-wing shots are closer) plus one faceoff-artifact cell. Within location cells: nothing.
- The royal-road proxy (prior same-team event ≤3 s, opposite side) captures only 4.3% of shots, 85% of which are rebound chains, not passes. Clean cross-seam one-timers have no recorded prior event (median time-since-event is 10 s) and are invisible in pbp.
- What did confirm: detectable cross-ice quick plays are devastating for everyone — ~14.7% conversion, ~25% of on-net shots scoring — but wing-neutral within the group. Pre-shot movement is the danger mechanism; whether handedness amplifies it requires pass data (Sznajder tracking, EDGE sprites, or manual video).
